Quick Start Guide 🚀

Three ways to get jitter data. Pick whichever fits your workflow.

A

📷 Live Camera

Use your webcam (or your stereo rig camera) to capture MediaPipe landmarks in real-time.

  1. Scroll down to Capture and select Live Camera
  2. Wait for the model to load (~4 MB, one-time download)
  3. Click Start Camera — grant camera permission if asked
  4. You'll see your skeleton overlay and live jitter numbers
  5. Click Record when ready, stand still or move as needed
  6. Click Stop Rec then Analyze Recording
  7. Full stats, charts, and export buttons appear below ✅
💡 Pro tip: Use a tripod and consistent lighting. The app requests 1080p for maximum accuracy.
B

🎬 Upload Video

Already recorded a video? Drop it in and let MediaPipe process every frame.

  1. Select the Upload Video tab
  2. Drag & drop your video (MP4, WebM, MOV)
  3. Watch the progress bar as each frame is analyzed
  4. Click Analyze Results when done
💡 Pro tip: Shorter clips process faster. A 30-second clip at 30fps = 900 frames.
C

📊 Upload CSV

Got data from your stereo vision rig or an exported MediaPipe run? Drop the CSV here.

  1. Select the Upload CSV tab
  2. Drop or browse for your CSV file
  3. Set shoulder mode (Trial Median is recommended)
  4. Confirm shoulder joint names match your column headers
  5. Click Run Analysis
💡 Your CSV needs these columns: source, domain, trial, frame, joint, x, y — and optionally z. See the methodology section for what each field means.

The Math (Don't Worry, It's Simple) 🧮

Here's exactly what happens under the hood when we compute jitter. Four steps, that's it.

01

Measure Movement

For each body landmark (wrist, elbow, etc.), we measure how far it moved between two consecutive frames. That's just Euclidean distance in whatever coordinate space you're in.

d(f) = ‖p(f) − p(f−1)‖₂
02

Measure Shoulder Width

We also compute your shoulder width in the same frame using the same units. This becomes our "ruler" to normalize against — because a person far from the camera looks smaller but shouldn't register more jitter.

w(f) = ‖shoulder_R − shoulder_L‖
03

Normalize

Divide the displacement by the median shoulder width for the trial. Now you get a dimensionless number — "0.02 shoulder widths of jitter per frame." This works in any coordinate space.

J(f) = d(f) / median(w)
04

Compare Across Systems

MediaPipe gives coordinates in 0–1 normalized space. Your stereo rig gives millimeters. But since both jitter values are in "shoulder widths," you can directly compare them. No fake unit conversion needed.

J_mediapipe ↔ J_stereo (same unit!)
"Jitter was computed as the Euclidean displacement of each landmark between consecutive frames within its native coordinate domain. Each displacement was divided by shoulder width measured in the same domain, yielding a dimensionless, body-relative metric suitable for cross-system comparison."
